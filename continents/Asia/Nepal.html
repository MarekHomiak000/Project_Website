Geography

Himalayan Mountains

Nepal is a landlocked country in South Asia, nestled between China and India. It is home to eight of the world’s 14 highest peaks, including Mount Everest, the tallest mountain on Earth.

Terrains from Hills to Plains

The country has diverse landscapes: the northern high Himalayas, the mid-hills with terraced farming, and the flat Terai plains in the south that support agriculture and wildlife.

History

Early Kingdoms

Nepal has a long history of small kingdoms and principalities, with the Kathmandu Valley being a cultural and political center for centuries.

Unification and Monarchy

In the 18th century, King Prithvi Narayan Shah unified Nepal into a single nation. The Shah monarchy continued until the 21st century, playing a central role in governance and national identity.

Transition to Republic

After political unrest and a decade-long civil conflict, Nepal became a federal democratic republic in 2008, abolishing the monarchy and establishing a new constitution in 2015.

Government

Federal Democratic Republic

Nepal is a federal parliamentary republic with a President as the ceremonial head of state and a Prime Minister as the head of government. It consists of seven provinces, each with its own governance structure.

Population

Ethnic and Linguistic Diversity

Nepal has approximately 30 million people, comprising multiple ethnic groups including Brahmins, Chhetris, Newars, Tamangs, and Tharus. Nepali is the official language, alongside dozens of regional languages.

Culture

Religion and Spirituality

Hinduism is the dominant religion, followed by Buddhism. Temples, stupas, and monasteries are integral to daily life, with festivals such as Dashain, Tihar, and Buddha Jayanti celebrated nationwide.

Art, Music, and Dance

Nepalese culture is rich in traditional music, dance, woodcarving, and Thangka paintings. Folk dances and rituals vary by region and ethnicity.

Economy

Agriculture and Remittances

Agriculture employs the majority of Nepal’s population, with rice, maize, and wheat as staple crops. Remittances from Nepalese working abroad significantly support the economy.

Tourism and Hydropower

Tourism, especially trekking and mountaineering, is a major source of income. Nepal’s rivers and topography also provide potential for hydropower generation.

Cuisine

Staple Dishes

Nepalese cuisine includes dal bhat (lentil soup with rice), momo (dumplings), and gundruk (fermented leafy greens). Meals often incorporate seasonal vegetables and local spices.

Regional Flavors

Cuisine varies regionally: mountain areas favor hearty foods, while the Terai plains include Indian-influenced dishes with more spices and flatbreads.

Wildlife

Himalayan Species

The high Himalayas host snow leopards, Himalayan tahr, red pandas, and musk deer. These species are adapted to cold, rugged terrain and alpine forests.

Terai Plains and Jungles

The southern Terai region is home to Bengal tigers, one-horned rhinoceros, elephants, and swamp deer, with Chitwan and Bardia National Parks protecting these endangered species.

Tourism

Trekking and Mountaineering

Nepal is famous for trekking trails like the Everest Base Camp, Annapurna Circuit, and Langtang Valley, attracting adventure tourists worldwide.

Cultural and Religious Sites

Historic cities such as Kathmandu, Bhaktapur, and Patan feature temples, palaces, and UNESCO World Heritage sites, reflecting Nepal’s rich cultural heritage.

Fun Facts

Home of Mount Everest

Nepal shares Mount Everest with China, the world’s highest peak at 8,848 meters, attracting climbers from around the globe.

Biodiversity Hotspot

Despite its small size, Nepal is one of the most ecologically diverse countries in the world, hosting ecosystems from tropical plains to alpine forests.
