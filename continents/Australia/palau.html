Geography

Island Nation in the Pacific

Palau is an archipelago of over 500 islands located in the western Pacific Ocean, part of the Micronesia region. The largest island, Babeldaob, hosts most of the population and government facilities.

Coral Reefs and Lagoons

The islands are surrounded by rich coral reefs, lagoons, and marine channels, making Palau one of the most biodiverse marine environments in the world.

History

Early Settlers

Palau was first settled by Micronesian peoples thousands of years ago, developing complex societies with stone structures, traditional governance, and navigation skills.

Colonial Period

Palau was influenced by Spain, Germany, and Japan before becoming part of the United States-administered Trust Territory of the Pacific Islands after World War II.

Independence

Palau became fully independent in 1994 under a Compact of Free Association with the United States, gaining sovereignty while maintaining close ties for defense and economic aid.

Government

Presidential Republic

Palau is a presidential republic with a President serving as both head of state and head of government. The Olbiil Era Kelulau, Palau’s legislature, enacts laws and oversees governance.

Population

Small and Diverse

Palau has around 18,000 residents, primarily Palauan, with smaller groups of Filipinos, Chinese, and other Pacific Islanders. English and Palauan are the official languages, while several indigenous languages are also spoken.

Culture

Traditional Practices

Palauan culture emphasizes family, clan systems, and respect for elders. Ceremonial rituals, storytelling, and traditional dances preserve heritage and identity.

Art and Craftsmanship

Stone monoliths, traditional canoe building, weaving, and tattooing reflect Palau’s artistic traditions, often used in cultural festivals and ceremonies.

Economy

Tourism

Tourism is the main economic driver, centered around scuba diving, snorkeling, and eco-tourism in Palau’s pristine marine and forest environments.

Fishing and Imports

Commercial fishing and subsistence fishing are important, while Palau imports many goods due to its small domestic production capacity.

Cuisine

Seafood and Local Staples

Palauan cuisine relies heavily on seafood such as tuna, reef fish, and shellfish. Coconut, taro, breadfruit, and tropical fruits are common ingredients.

Influences from Abroad

Cuisine also shows influences from Japanese, Filipino, and American foods due to historical connections and modern trade.

Wildlife

Marine Life

Palau is renowned for its marine biodiversity, including coral reefs, sharks, manta rays, sea turtles, and countless tropical fish species.

Terrestrial Animals

The islands are home to birds like the Palau fruit dove and Micronesian kingfisher, as well as monitor lizards and endemic bats, inhabiting forests and mangroves.

Tourism

Scuba Diving and Snorkeling

Tourists visit Palau for world-class diving spots like the Blue Corner, Jellyfish Lake, and German Channel, exploring its rich coral reefs and marine biodiversity.

Cultural and Eco-Tourism

Visitors also explore traditional villages, stone monoliths, and nature reserves, participating in cultural tours and conservation activities.

Fun Facts

Jellyfish Lake

Palau’s Jellyfish Lake is home to millions of harmless golden jellyfish, offering a unique snorkeling experience unlike anywhere else in the world.

Marine Sanctuary

Palau was the first country to establish a shark sanctuary, banning commercial shark fishing to protect its ocean ecosystems.
